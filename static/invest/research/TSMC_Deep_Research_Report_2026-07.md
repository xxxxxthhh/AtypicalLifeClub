## 1. Business Overview

TSMC is the world's most important pure-play foundry. Its core product is not simply chip production. It combines leading process technology, yield, IP / EDA ecosystem, customer confidentiality, and scale manufacturing into a manufacturing platform that is extremely hard to replace. In the AI-infrastructure chain, TSMC links NVIDIA, AMD, Broadcom, Marvell, Apple, Qualcomm, MediaTek, and other design customers with equipment, materials, packaging, substrates, power, and water.

| Business / capability | Current readout | AI-infra meaning |
|-----------------------|-----------------|------------------|
| Advanced nodes | ~~7nm and below were about 74% of wafer revenue in 2026Q1~~ **77% in 2026Q2** | Manufacturing core for AI accelerators, CPUs, ASICs, and networking chips |
| HPC platform | ~~About 61% of 2026Q1 revenue~~ **66% of 2026Q2 revenue** | AI/HPC is now the main revenue structure, not just a narrative |
| N3 / N2 roadmap | ~~3nm about 25%, 5nm about 36%; N2 ramping but not yet revenue-recognized~~ **2nm 3%, 3nm 30%, and 5nm 33% of Q2 wafer revenue** | Next GPU / ASIC migration drives supply elasticity and margin |
| Advanced packaging | CoWoS / 3DFabric is one of the AI accelerator bottlenecks | Links HBM, GPU / ASIC die, and system-level packages |
| Global manufacturing | Taiwan core footprint plus Arizona, Japan, and Germany expansion | Helps localization and geopolitical acceptance, but raises cost |

TSMC matters because it validates whether design-company orders can become real supply. NVIDIA can sell more GPUs, Broadcom can win more XPU programs, and Marvell can win more custom ASIC sockets, but all of those stories must eventually pass through TSMC wafer starts, advanced packaging, and delivery cadence.

## 2. Industry & Competitive Position

The AI-infrastructure cycle is pulling foundry from the background to the foreground. GPUs, XPUs, AI networking ASICs, CPUs, and high-end mobile SoCs are competing for the same advanced processes and packaging capacity. TSMC's bargaining power has three layers.

First, advanced-node leadership. ~~Advanced nodes were about 74% of wafer revenue in 2026Q1, and 3nm / 5nm together were about 61%.~~ **2026-07-16 update:** 7nm-and-below reached 77% of Q2 wafer revenue, with 2nm at 3%, 3nm at 30%, and 5nm at 33%. AI-chip growth is concentrated in the process bands where TSMC has the most pricing power.

Second, ecosystem lock-in. NVIDIA, Apple, AMD, Broadcom, Qualcomm, and other customers are not merely buying wafers. Their multiyear product roadmaps, IP, EDA flows, packaging choices, and yield ramps are tied to TSMC. Switching cost is not just price; it is launch timing and yield risk.

Third, advanced packaging has become a second bottleneck. AI-accelerator supply increasingly depends on CoWoS / 3DFabric, HBM supply, and substrates. TSMC is not just controlling wafers; it also controls key back-end capacity.

| Competitor set | TSMC advantage | TSMC risk |
|----------------|----------------|-----------|
| Intel Foundry | Proven yield, customer trust, and ecosystem | If Intel 18A / 14A wins second-source programs, marginal pricing pressure rises |
| Samsung Foundry | More stable advanced-node execution and flagship customer trust | Samsung can use price and the Korean ecosystem to win some AI ASIC work |
| In-house / packaging alternatives | TSMC still controls leading-edge logic | Customers may diversify packaging, back end, or mature-node work to reduce dependence |
| Geopolitical local fabs | Arizona / Japan / Germany improve customer acceptance | Overseas fabs carry higher labor, construction, and ramp costs |

Neocloud capex depends more on customer demand, financing, and utilization. Still, if AI demand cools, TSMC's high fixed cost and N3 / N2 / overseas expansion can create cyclical pressure.

## 4. Management & Governance

TSMC's governance advantage is long-termism and engineering discipline. After C.C. Wei became both chairman and CEO, the company continued to execute around customer roadmaps, yield, capacity discipline, and capex cadence. Management has acknowledged AI-bubble risk while emphasizing careful expansion. That matters for a foundry because overcapacity hurts a manufacturer more directly than it hurts a fabless company.

The risk is the same centrality. TSMC sits at the intersection of Taiwan's economy, the global AI supply chain, and U.S. / China / Japan / Europe industrial policy. Geopolitical conflict, export controls, customer localization requirements, chemical or gas supply shocks, and energy / water constraints can all be amplified through valuation and supply-chain stability.

## 5. Bull Case

The bull case is that TSMC is the most reliable tax layer on AI capex.

1. **AI demand is already in the revenue mix.** HPC was 66% of 2026Q2 revenue (+20% QoQ), so this is not only customer guidance.
2. **Advanced-node pricing power is increasing.** 3nm, 5nm, and 7nm remain heavily utilized, and public reports indicate TSMC is raising advanced-node prices.
3. **N3 / N2 migration creates multiyear growth.** Next-generation GPUs and ASICs moving from N5 / N4 into N3 / N2 should keep consuming scarce capacity.
4. **Advanced packaging is a second moat.** CoWoS / 3DFabric means TSMC controls not only wafers but also AI-system packaging cadence.
5. **Net cash and high FCF reduce cycle risk.** Even with high capex, TSMC has strong cash and operating cash flow.

Upside context: if 2026 revenue reaches or exceeds the roughly $158B public frame, gross margin stays above 60%, and CoWoS / N3 / N2 remain tight, the market may continue treating TSMC as the safest manufacturing-layer beneficiary of AI infrastructure.

## 6. Bear Case

The bear case is that TSMC is so good that the valuation now requires sustained excellence.

1. **Valuation tolerance is lower.** A $2.25T market cap and about 14x 2026E EV/Sales is not cheap for a capital-intensive manufacturer.
2. **Customer concentration is rising.** Public reports indicate NVIDIA has become one of the largest customers; AI-accelerator scheduling swings would reach the foundry quickly.
3. **Overseas expansion can pressure margins.** Arizona, Japan, and Germany improve geopolitical resilience, but their cost structures are higher than Taiwan.
4. **N2 ramp brings depreciation and yield pressure.** Early new-node ramps usually weigh on margin, even if long-term returns are attractive.
5. **Geopolitical and resource risks are non-trivial.** Taiwan power, water, earthquakes, cross-strait risk, and chemical / gas supply can all become supply risks.

Downside context: if AI customer order cadence slows, N3 / N2 expansion arrives before real demand, or overseas fab costs exceed pricing pass-through, the market can re-rate TSMC from scarce growth manufacturer to high-capex cyclical manufacturer.

**What's priced in & the expectation gap:** At the $434.16 ADR close on Jul 2, 2026, TSMC traded at roughly 14x 2026E EV/Sales. If that premium multiple normalizes toward about 10x over three years and the required return is 8%, the reverse-multiple frame implies about `(14 / 10)^(1/3) x 1.08 - 1`, or roughly **21% revenue CAGR**. Our base case is close to that, not clearly above it: revenue growth above 30% in 2026, then about 20-22% through 2028 with gross margin still mostly above 60%. That leaves quality very high but the expectation gap balanced at the current dated price.

TSMC should not be valued only with a legacy foundry-cycle frame, but it should not get NVIDIA's fabless premium either. It is closer to a long-duration option on global leading-edge logic scarcity. The risk is that the option is no longer cheap.

## 10. Conclusion

TSMC is necessary coverage for the foundry layer. Its chain-validation job is to tell us whether AI-infrastructure demand is truly reaching the upstream manufacturing bottleneck. If monthly revenue, advanced-node mix, N3 / N2, and advanced packaging stay tight, the AI capex narrative is more credible. If those indicators loosen, design-company growth and neocloud backlog both need recalibration.

The expectation gap is balanced: at a $434.16 ADR and roughly 14x 2026E EV/Sales, the market is underwriting about a 21% three-year revenue CAGR with 60%+ gross margin; our base case is close rather than clearly above it.

The current stance is **neutral watch, medium conviction**. The 30% bull / 50% base / 20% bear grid says the company is still exceptional, but the base case is already mostly capitalized at the current dated price. Neutral is therefore not a lack of view: it is the conclusion that TSMC's quality and its valuation are currently in rough balance.
