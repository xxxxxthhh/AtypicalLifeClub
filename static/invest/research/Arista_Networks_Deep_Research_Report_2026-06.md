## Executive Summary

**Current judgment:** **Neutral watch / medium conviction: high quality, mostly priced in.** I would rather own ANET through a valuation reset or after clearer proof that open Ethernet is taking durable AI back-end share than chase it purely because the narrative is right. The company has the financial profile we want in this book: fast growth, very high margins, large net cash, deep hyperscaler relevance, and a direct read on AI cluster architecture. But the June 29, 2026 price already underwrites multiple years of AI network expansion and excellent execution.

## 1. Business Overview

Arista sells cloud networking systems and software for large-scale data centers, AI clusters, enterprises, campuses, routing, and WAN environments. The company reports one operating segment, but its strategic language breaks the market into "Centers of Data": AI Centers, Data Centers, Campus Centers, and WAN Centers.

The product stack has four layers that matter:

| Layer | Role in the stack |
|----|----|
| Switching and routing platforms | High-capacity switches, routers, AI spine/leaf platforms, and data-center systems |
| EOS | Linux-based, state-oriented network operating system designed to run consistently across platforms |
| CloudVision / NetDL / AVA | Network telemetry, automation, data-lake observability, and AI-assisted operations |
| Etherlink / AI networking platforms | 800G systems, AI spine/leaf designs, virtual output queues, large buffers, load balancing, and congestion-management features |

In Q1 2026, product revenue was **$2.311B** and service revenue was **$398M**. That split is important: Arista is still primarily a systems company, not a pure SaaS company. The software layer matters because it strengthens customer lock-in and operational reliability, but the near-term revenue engine remains high-performance networking hardware.

The business is also deeply hyperscaler-linked. In FY2025, Cloud and AI Titans were **48%** of revenue, AI and Specialty Providers were **20%**, and Enterprise was **32%**. Product-category mix tells the same story: Core AI/Cloud/Data Center Networking was **65%** of revenue, Cognitive Adjacencies were **18%**, and Software and Services were **17%**.

## 2. Industry & Competitive Position

AI training and inference clusters are not only accelerator problems. A cluster with thousands to hundreds of thousands of GPUs, XPUs, or custom accelerators becomes a networked computer. If the fabric drops packets, adds latency, or fails under congestion, expensive compute sits idle. That is why back-end AI networking has become a strategic battleground rather than a normal enterprise-networking refresh cycle.

Arista's FY2025 10-K frames AI networking across three architecture layers: **Scale Up** connects accelerators in close proximity; **Scale Out** connects accelerators across racks; **Scale Across** connects clusters, data centers, and regions. Scale Up remains more proprietary today, while Scale Out and Scale Across are the clearest places for open Ethernet to challenge InfiniBand-style proprietary approaches.

### 2.1 NVIDIA: architectural competitor, not just a supplier comparison

NVIDIA owns the strongest integrated AI compute stack: GPUs, CUDA, systems, InfiniBand, NVLink/NVSwitch, and Spectrum-X Ethernet. Arista is the alternative expression: best-of-breed open Ethernet with Arista software and merchant silicon. The issue is not whether both can grow. The issue is where the marginal dollar of AI cluster networking value accrues.

NVIDIA wins if customers value full-stack optimization and the fastest route to working clusters more than openness. ANET wins if hyperscalers and AI builders want supplier diversity, Ethernet standardization, operational control, and a fabric that can work across multiple accelerator types.

### 2.2 Broadcom: supplier/partner first, hidden risk second

Arista's FY2025 10-K says the company primarily relies on Broadcom for merchant switching chips. That means ANET is a strong customer and ecosystem validator for Broadcom. It also means the two are not clean horizontal competitors. Broadcom monetizes silicon; Arista monetizes systems, EOS, CloudVision, customer deployment, and operational reliability.

This is powerful, but it is also a risk. Arista's roadmap is partly constrained by merchant silicon availability, pricing, and cadence. If Broadcom supply tightens, if a silicon generation slips, or if hyperscalers increasingly internalize switching system design, ANET's margin and growth narrative can change.

### 2.3 Optical, power and demand-chain cross-checks

AAOI covers active optical modules/transceivers. Corning covers fiber, cable, and passive optical connectivity/materials. ANET sits above those layers as a systems and fabric vendor. More 800G AI spines and scale-out networks imply more optical links, more fiber/cable density, and more data-center connectivity content. ANET therefore gives us an architectural demand signal for the optical stack, not a duplicate AAOI/Corning thesis.

Vertiv and CoreWeave complete the cross-check. VRT tells us whether data-center power and cooling equipment is moving through the physical deployment pipeline. CRWV tests whether part of AI demand is being financed through high leverage and circular commitments. ANET sits between them: it should show whether the actual compute clusters are being wired and brought online.

### 3.2 Balance sheet, cash flow and visibility

At March 31, 2026, Arista had about **$2.79B** of cash and equivalents and **$9.56B** of marketable securities, or about **$12.35B** combined. The company has no meaningful financial debt burden. Q1 2026 capex was only about **$55M**, implying very high free-cash-flow conversion for the quarter.

Deferred revenue and remaining performance obligations are important watch items. Current deferred revenue was about **$4.91B** and non-current deferred revenue was about **$1.29B** at March 31, 2026. The company also disclosed about **$7.7B** of future revenue from contract liabilities, deferred revenue, and other performance obligations, with about **91%** expected to be recognized over the next two years. That gives visibility, but it is not the same as an unconditional backlog; acceptance periods, deployment timing, and customer architecture decisions still matter.

### 3.3 Guidance and quality checks

The Q2 2026 guide calls for about **$2.8B** of revenue and **46-47%** non-GAAP operating margin, with non-GAAP diluted EPS around **$0.88**. Against Q2 2025 revenue of **$2.205B**, that implies roughly **27%** year-over-year growth - still robust, but a step down from Q1 2026's **35.1%** - while maintaining very high profitability.

The main financial quality issue is not leverage. It is concentration and timing. Arista disclosed that two anonymous end customers were **26%** and **16%** of FY2025 revenue, or **42%** combined. The company does not officially name them, and this report should stay with the disclosed anonymous concentration rather than presenting market speculation as fact. At that concentration level, architecture shifts or capex pauses by either customer matter a lot.

## 4. Management & Governance

Jayshree Ullal remains Chief Executive Officer and Chairperson. Kenneth Duda is President and Chief Technology Officer. Chantelle Breithaupt is Chief Financial Officer. Todd Nightingale is President and Chief Operating Officer. This is a deeply technical leadership team, and ANET's operating record shows strong execution discipline.

Management quality is a positive part of the thesis. Arista has repeatedly combined fast revenue growth with very high margins and strong cash generation. The main governance/ownership issue for investors is not obvious self-dealing; it is whether management can keep navigating hyperscaler concentration, merchant-silicon dependence, and AI architecture transitions without overbuilding inventory or compromising margin.

The Q4/FY2025 release also highlighted operational milestones that matter for credibility: cumulative shipments of **150M ports**, next-generation data/AI center networks, AI operations tools, ESUN participation, and the VeloCloud SD-WAN portfolio acquired from Broadcom. Those do not eliminate execution risk, but they support the view that Arista is one of the few vendors with both technical depth and hyperscaler operating trust.

## 5. Bull Case

1. **Open Ethernet wins more AI back-end networking share.** If large cloud and AI customers standardize around Ethernet for scale-out clusters, Arista has a strong right to win. EOS, CloudVision, large-buffer spine platforms, and high-speed Ethernet systems are directly aligned with that architecture.

2. **Hyperscalers prefer supplier diversity and operational control.** Customers with massive internal engineering teams often dislike being locked into one vertically integrated stack. Arista benefits when customers want to mix accelerators, standardize operations, and keep networking choices modular.

3. **Broadcom merchant silicon remains the ecosystem center of gravity.** If Broadcom continues to deliver competitive switching silicon on time, ANET can keep converting silicon capability into finished systems and software value without carrying full custom-silicon risk.

4. **Software and telemetry deepen lock-in.** CloudVision, NetDL, and AI-assisted operations are not the same as pure SaaS, but they help make Arista harder to displace once a network is deployed and operational processes are built around it.

5. **The AI optical stack becomes a structural pull-through.** More 800G and future higher-speed deployments should support AAOI-like active optics demand and Corning-like fiber/cable/passive connectivity demand. ANET becomes a leading indicator for that pull-through.

## 6. Bear Case

1. **NVIDIA's integrated stack wins more value.** If Spectrum-X, InfiniBand, NVLink/NVSwitch, and NVIDIA systems become the default AI cluster architecture, Arista can still grow, but its share of the highest-value AI networking opportunity may be smaller than bulls expect.

2. **Customer concentration is very high.** Two anonymous end customers were **26%** and **16%** of FY2025 revenue. At **42%** combined revenue, a capex pause, design shift, or share loss at either customer can move the consolidated growth rate.

3. **Broadcom dependence cuts both ways.** Broadcom is an ecosystem strength and a supplier concentration risk. Arista has no guaranteed long-term silicon supply, and its product roadmap depends on close collaboration with merchant silicon vendors.

4. **Valuation leaves little room for ordinary disappointment.** At about **20x EV/TTM revenue** and roughly **47x current non-GAAP run-rate EPS**, a normal deceleration can still hurt the stock even if the company remains excellent.

5. **White-box, in-house, and custom-networking pressure remains real.** Large cloud customers have the scale to disaggregate, self-design, or pressure system margins. Arista's moat is operational reliability and software depth, not immunity from customer bargaining power.

## 7. Key Uncertainties

1. **Ethernet vs NVIDIA integrated networking.** If NVIDIA's integrated stack becomes the default for large AI clusters, ANET's AI opportunity is narrower.

2. **Scale-up timing.** Scale Up remains more proprietary today. Ethernet's ability to move deeper into scale-up networks is still a future opportunity, not a fully proven current base case.

3. **Top-customer behavior.** The two largest disclosed anonymous customers at **26%** and **16%** of FY2025 revenue can dominate quarterly optics, inventory, and deployment timing.

4. **RPO/deferred revenue durability.** The **$7.7B** future-revenue disclosure supports visibility, but recognition depends on customer deployment, acceptance, and architecture decisions.

5. **Broadcom silicon cadence.** Arista's AI systems depend on merchant silicon roadmaps. Delays, shortages, or pricing pressure would show up in product timing and margins.

6. **Margin mix.** AI-scale customers can be large and demanding. Non-GAAP operating margin falling below **40%** without a clear investment explanation would be a thesis warning.

7. **Inventory and purchase commitments.** Large purchase obligations can become a risk if AI demand timing changes after supply has been committed.

**What's priced in & the expectation gap:** At **$164.10** and about **20x EV/TTM revenue**, the market is not pricing ANET as a cyclical switch vendor. If a mature premium networking/software multiple is closer to **10-12x revenue** and investors require roughly **8%** annual return, a three-year reverse-multiple frame implies revenue needs to compound roughly `((20 / 12)^(1/3) x 1.08 - 1) ≈ 28%` to `((20 / 10)^(1/3) x 1.08 - 1) ≈ 36%` while margins stay exceptional. Our base case is near the low end of that implied range, not clearly above it: still strong growth and elite margins, but with concentration and architecture risk. The expectation gap is therefore balanced, not positive.

That valuation can be justified only if several things remain true at once: AI cluster networking demand remains strong, Ethernet wins meaningful share, Arista keeps hyperscaler trust, Broadcom merchant silicon remains available and competitive, and margins do not compress sharply as AI customers scale.

## 9. Catalysts & Monitoring Checklist

1. **Q2 2026 results and Q3 guide.** Revenue around or above **$2.8B** with **46-47%** non-GAAP operating margin keeps the story intact.

2. **Customer concentration.** Watch whether the top two customers remain near **40%+** of revenue and whether either accelerates or pauses materially.

3. **Deferred revenue/RPO.** Growth in the **$7.7B** future-revenue base supports visibility; weakness would challenge the AI buildout narrative.

4. **Ethernet vs NVIDIA networking.** Track customer architecture commentary, Spectrum-X adoption, InfiniBand displacement, and Ultra Ethernet/ESUN progress.

5. **Broadcom silicon cadence.** Any supply constraint, pricing pressure, or roadmap slip matters to Arista's AI system timing and margins.

6. **Optical pull-through.** ANET strength should show up in AAOI/Corning demand signals if AI network density continues to rise.

7. **Cash conversion.** Q1 2026 operating cash flow of **$1.69B** was unusually strong. Sustained free-cash-flow conversion would support the quality premium.

8. **Margin floor.** Non-GAAP operating margin below **40%** without a clear investment explanation would indicate AI mix, customer discounts, or competition are biting harder than expected.

## 10. Conclusion

ANET's chain-validation job is to tell us whether the networking layer is becoming an open Ethernet systems/software opportunity or a vertically integrated accelerator-stack opportunity. If Arista keeps expanding deferred revenue/RPO, sustaining 40%+ operating margins, and showing large AI customers choosing Ethernet fabrics, the open-Ethernet and Broadcom-merchant-silicon side of the book gets stronger. If those indicators weaken, the AI networking profit pool may be migrating toward NVIDIA's integrated stack.

The expectation gap is balanced: at $164.10 and about 20x EV/TTM revenue, the market is underwriting roughly high-20s to mid-30s revenue CAGR for several years with elite margins; our base case is close to the low end of that range, not clearly above it.

The current stance is **neutral watch, medium conviction**. The 30% bull / 50% base / 20% bear grid says ANET is an exceptional business, but the base case is already mostly capitalized at the dated price. Medium conviction reflects unusually clean reported margins, cash, and customer relevance, offset by the unresolved open-Ethernet vs integrated-stack architecture debate.

Upgrade trigger: move to constructive if Q2/Q3 results keep revenue growth above the high-20s, non-GAAP operating margin stays above 45%, deferred revenue/RPO expands, and customer evidence shows open Ethernet gaining AI back-end share without higher concentration. Downgrade trigger: move to cautious if revenue growth falls below the implied high-20s path, non-GAAP operating margin drops below 40% without clear investment explanation, top-customer concentration worsens, or NVIDIA integrated networking takes clearer AI back-end share.
