## Executive Summary

Arista Networks is the cleanest public-company expression of the open Ethernet side of the AI cluster networking debate. In this book's AI infrastructure map, ANET is not the GPU/accelerator layer, not the optical module layer, and not the fiber/cable layer. It is the switching systems, network operating system, telemetry, and cloud/AI data-center fabric layer that tests a very important question: will large AI clusters standardize around open Ethernet and merchant silicon, or will more value migrate into NVIDIA's vertically integrated networking stack?

That makes ANET a high-value cross-check against the existing coverage:

- Against NVIDIA: ANET is a competing architecture read. NVIDIA sells InfiniBand, NVLink/NVSwitch, Spectrum-X Ethernet, GPUs, and systems as an integrated stack; Arista sells open Ethernet switching systems and EOS/CloudVision software around merchant silicon.
- Against Broadcom: ANET is more partner/customer than direct rival. Broadcom is the primary merchant-silicon supplier behind much of the Ethernet switching ecosystem, while Arista captures system design, software, operations, and customer co-design value. This is powerful, but it creates supplier concentration risk.
- Against AAOI and Corning: ANET is upstream demand for higher-speed optical modules, fiber, cable, and passive connectivity. It is not a substitute for AAOI/GLW coverage; it explains why 800G/AI spine/scale-out networks pull more optical content through the stack.
- Against Vertiv and CoreWeave: ANET's order/revenue/RPO trajectory helps validate whether AI data centers are actually being built; CRWV tests whether demand is over-financed, and VRT tests whether physical power/cooling deployments are still accelerating.

The business quality is excellent. Q1 2026 revenue was $2.709B, up 35.1% year over year, with non-GAAP operating margin of 47.8%, non-GAAP EPS of $0.87, and operating cash flow of $1.69B. The balance sheet is very strong: cash plus marketable securities were about $12.35B at March 31, 2026, with no meaningful debt line. FY2025 revenue was $9.006B, up 28.6%, and Arista's FY2025 mix shows why the company belongs in the AI infrastructure book: Cloud and AI Titans were 48% of revenue, AI and Specialty Providers were 20%, and Core AI/Cloud/Data Center Networking was 65%.

The stock, however, already prices in a lot of that goodness. Using the June 29, 2026 end-of-day market cap of $206.62B and Q1 2026 cash/securities, enterprise value is about $194B. That is roughly 20x trailing twelve-month revenue, about 56x TTM GAAP net income, and about 47x current non-GAAP EPS run-rate. This is a high-quality compounder, but not a cheap one. At this valuation, the main question is not "is Arista real?" It is "how much open Ethernet share gain and AI cluster duration are already embedded in the stock?"

## Investment View

**Rating: Neutral / high-quality but high expectations (watch).**

I would rather own ANET through a valuation reset or a clear proof-point that open Ethernet is taking durable AI back-end share than chase it purely because the narrative is correct. The company has the financial profile we want in this book: fast growth, very high margins, large net cash, deep hyperscaler relevance, and a direct read on AI cluster architecture. But the valuation is already underwriting multiple years of AI network expansion and very high execution quality.

The most useful role of ANET in this coverage structure is therefore not only as a possible long. It is a dashboard name. If ANET continues to grow RPO/deferred revenue, sustain 40%+ operating margins, and show large AI customers choosing Ethernet fabrics at scale, that supports the bull case for Broadcom, AAOI, Corning, Vertiv, and parts of the neocloud demand chain. If ANET decelerates or shows architecture pressure from NVIDIA's integrated stack, it is an early warning that part of the AI infrastructure book may be overextended.

## Business Overview

Arista sells cloud networking systems and software for large-scale data centers, AI clusters, enterprises, campuses, routing, and WAN environments. The company reports one operating segment, but its strategic language breaks the market into "Centers of Data": AI Centers, Data Centers, Campus Centers, and WAN Centers.

The product stack has four parts that matter:

1. **Switching and routing platforms.** This is the hardware foundation: high-capacity switches, routers, AI spine/leaf platforms, and data-center systems.
2. **EOS.** Arista's Extensible Operating System is a Linux-based, state-oriented network OS designed to run consistently across platforms.
3. **CloudVision / NetDL / AVA.** These software layers give customers network telemetry, automation, data-lake observability, and AI-assisted operations.
4. **Etherlink / AI networking platforms.** This is the core AI cluster effort, including 800G systems, AI spine/leaf designs, and features such as virtual output queues, large buffers, load balancing, and congestion-management tools.

In Q1 2026, product revenue was $2.311B and service revenue was $398M. That product/service split is important: Arista is still primarily a systems company, not a SaaS company. The software layer matters because it strengthens customer lock-in and operating reliability, but the near-term revenue engine is high-performance networking hardware.

## Why ANET Belongs In The AI Infrastructure Book

AI training and inference clusters are not only accelerator problems. A cluster with thousands to hundreds of thousands of GPUs, XPUs, or custom accelerators becomes a networked computer. If the fabric drops packets, adds latency, or fails under congestion, expensive compute sits idle. That is why back-end AI networking has become a strategic battleground rather than a normal enterprise-networking refresh cycle.

Arista's opportunity is to make Ethernet good enough, open enough, and operationally mature enough to be the default AI scale-out fabric for large customers. Its FY2025 10-K frames AI networking across three architecture layers:

- **Scale Up:** connecting accelerators within close proximity. This is still heavily proprietary today, but Arista sees open Ethernet standards as a future opportunity.
- **Scale Out:** connecting accelerators across racks. This is where Ethernet can attack InfiniBand-style proprietary approaches.
- **Scale Across:** connecting clusters, data centers, and regions.

This is exactly the unresolved question that ANET helps us underwrite. If the world converges toward open Ethernet fabrics, Arista benefits directly and Broadcom benefits through merchant silicon. If customers prefer tighter accelerator-plus-network integration, NVIDIA captures more of the networking value pool.

## Relationship To Existing Coverage

### NVIDIA: architectural competitor, not just a supplier comparison

NVIDIA owns the strongest integrated AI compute stack: GPUs, CUDA, systems, InfiniBand, NVLink/NVSwitch, and Spectrum-X Ethernet. ANET is the alternative expression: best-of-breed open Ethernet with Arista software and merchant silicon. The issue is not whether both can grow. The issue is where the marginal dollar of AI cluster networking value accrues.

NVIDIA wins if customers value full-stack optimization and the fastest route to working clusters more than openness. ANET wins if hyperscalers and AI builders want supplier diversity, Ethernet standardization, operational control, and a fabric that can work across multiple accelerator types.

### Broadcom: supplier/partner first, hidden risk second

Arista's FY2025 10-K says the company primarily relies on Broadcom for merchant switching chips. That means ANET is a strong customer and ecosystem validator for Broadcom. It also means the two are not clean horizontal competitors. Broadcom monetizes silicon; Arista monetizes the system, EOS, CloudVision, and customer deployment layer.

The risk is that Arista's roadmap is partly constrained by merchant silicon availability, pricing, and cadence. If Broadcom supply tightens, if a silicon generation slips, or if hyperscalers increasingly internalize switching system design, ANET's margin and growth narrative can change.

### AAOI and Corning: downstream demand chain, not direct overlap

AAOI covers active optical modules/transceivers. Corning covers fiber, cable, and passive optical connectivity/materials. ANET sits above those layers as a systems and fabric vendor. More 800G AI spines and scale-out networks imply more optical links, more fiber/cable density, and more data-center connectivity content. ANET therefore gives us an architectural demand signal for the optical stack.

This distinction matters. Calling both AAOI and ANET "optical interconnect" coverage would blur the stack. AAOI/Corning are optical-content names; Arista is the network-fabric control point that can pull that content through customer deployments.

### Vertiv and CoreWeave: physical buildout and demand-risk cross-checks

VRT tells us whether data-center power and cooling equipment is moving through the physical deployment pipeline. CRWV tells us whether part of AI demand is being financed through high leverage and circular commitments. ANET sits between them: it should show whether the actual compute clusters are being wired and brought online. Together, these names create a healthier coverage structure than a one-way AI capex bull case.

## Bull Case

**1. Open Ethernet wins more AI back-end networking share.**
If large cloud and AI customers standardize around Ethernet for scale-out clusters, Arista has a strong right to win. EOS, CloudVision, large-buffer spine platforms, and high-speed Ethernet systems are directly aligned with that architecture.

**2. Hyperscalers prefer supplier diversity and operational control.**
Customers with massive internal engineering teams often dislike being locked into one vertically integrated stack. Arista benefits when customers want to mix accelerators, standardize operations, and keep networking choices modular.

**3. Broadcom merchant silicon remains the ecosystem center of gravity.**
If Broadcom continues to deliver competitive switching silicon on time, ANET can keep converting silicon capability into finished systems and software value without carrying full custom-silicon risk.

**4. Software and telemetry deepen lock-in.**
CloudVision, NetDL, and AI-assisted operations are not the same as pure SaaS, but they help make Arista harder to displace once a network is deployed and operational processes are built around it.

**5. The AI optical stack becomes a structural pull-through.**
More 800G and future higher-speed deployments should support AAOI-like active optics demand and Corning-like fiber/cable/passive connectivity demand. ANET becomes a leading indicator for that pull-through.

## Bear Case

**1. NVIDIA's integrated stack wins more value.**
If Spectrum-X, InfiniBand, NVLink/NVSwitch, and NVIDIA systems become the default AI cluster architecture, Arista can still grow, but its share of the highest-value AI networking opportunity may be smaller than bulls expect.

**2. Customer concentration is very high.**
Arista disclosed that two anonymous end customers were 26% and 16% of FY2025 revenue. The company does not officially name them. Market speculation may point to hyperscaler identities, but the official report should stay with the disclosed anonymous concentration. At 42% combined revenue, architecture shifts or capex pauses by either customer matter a lot.

**3. Broadcom dependence cuts both ways.**
Broadcom is an ecosystem strength and a supplier concentration risk. Arista has no guaranteed long-term silicon supply, and its product roadmap depends on close collaboration with merchant silicon vendors.

**4. Valuation leaves little room for ordinary disappointment.**
At about 20x EV/TTM revenue and roughly 47x current non-GAAP run-rate EPS, a normal deceleration can still hurt the stock even if the company remains excellent.

**5. White-box, in-house, and custom-networking pressure remains real.**
Large cloud customers have the scale to disaggregate, self-design, or pressure system margins. Arista's moat is operational reliability and software depth, not immunity from customer bargaining power.

## Management And Governance

Jayshree Ullal remains Chief Executive Officer and Chairperson. Kenneth Duda is President and Chief Technology Officer. Chantelle Breithaupt is Chief Financial Officer. Todd Nightingale is President and Chief Operating Officer. This is a deeply technical leadership team, and ANET's operating record shows strong execution discipline.

Management quality is a positive part of the thesis. Arista has repeatedly combined fast revenue growth with very high margins and strong cash generation. The main governance/ownership issue for investors is not obvious self-dealing; it is whether management can keep navigating hyperscaler concentration, merchant-silicon dependence, and AI architecture transitions without overbuilding inventory or compromising margin.

## Key Monitoring Items

1. **Q2 2026 results and Q3 guide.** Revenue around or above $2.8B with 46-47% non-GAAP operating margin keeps the story intact.
2. **Customer concentration.** Watch whether the top two customers remain near 40%+ of revenue and whether any one customer accelerates or pauses materially.
3. **Deferred revenue/RPO.** Growth in the $7.7B future-revenue base supports visibility; weakness would challenge the AI buildout narrative.
4. **Ethernet vs NVIDIA networking.** Track customer architecture commentary, Spectrum-X adoption, InfiniBand displacement, and Ultra Ethernet/ESUN progress.
5. **Broadcom silicon cadence.** Any supply constraint, pricing pressure, or roadmap slip matters to Arista's AI system timing and margins.
6. **Optical pull-through.** ANET strength should show up in AAOI/Corning demand signals if AI network density continues to rise.
7. **Inventory and purchase commitments.** FY2025 purchase obligations were large. If demand timing changes, inventory risk can appear quickly.
8. **Margins.** Non-GAAP operating margin below 40% without a clear investment explanation would be a thesis warning.

## What Would Change The View

I would become more constructive if valuation reset to a level that leaves room for normal execution volatility, or if Arista demonstrates that Ethernet is winning the majority of new large-scale AI back-end network deployments across multiple customers and accelerator types while margins stay above 40%.

I would become more negative if NVIDIA's integrated networking stack becomes the clear default for AI clusters, if one of the two largest customers slows materially, if deferred revenue/RPO stops growing while product revenue remains elevated, or if Broadcom dependence turns into a supply or pricing bottleneck.

## Conclusion

ANET is a necessary addition to the AI infrastructure coverage map because it tells us whether the networking layer is becoming an open Ethernet systems/software opportunity or a vertically integrated accelerator-stack opportunity. It is a high-quality company with a strong balance sheet, exceptional margins, and direct exposure to AI cluster buildouts.

But the stock already understands much of that. At roughly $206.6B of market cap, about $164 per share, about 20x EV/TTM revenue, and about 47x current non-GAAP run-rate EPS, the burden of proof sits with the bulls. For now, the best stance is neutral/watch: own the quality of the business intellectually, but demand either a better price or stronger proof that open Ethernet share gains are still underappreciated.
