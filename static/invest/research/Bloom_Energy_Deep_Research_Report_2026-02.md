## 1. Business Overview

### What Bloom Energy Does

Bloom Energy designs, manufactures, and sells solid oxide fuel cell (SOFC) systems â€” called "Bloom Energy Servers" â€” that convert natural gas, biogas, or hydrogen into electricity through an electrochemical process, without combustion. The technology originated from NASA Mars exploration research.

### Revenue Model

Bloom operates a hybrid hardware + services model:

- **Product Revenue (~70%):** Sale of Energy Server systems. Average deal sizes have grown dramatically with data center contracts.
- **Service Revenue (~30%):** Long-term maintenance and monitoring contracts, typically 10-20 year agreements. This creates a recurring revenue base that grows with installed capacity.
- **Emerging: Electrolyzers** â€” Bloom's SOEC (Solid Oxide Electrolyzer Cell) technology runs the fuel cell process in reverse to produce hydrogen. Still early-stage commercially.

| Emissions | ~50% less CO2 vs grid | Varies | High |
| 24/7 Reliability | Yes | Grid-dependent | Backup only |

### The AI Data Center Pivot

This is the defining narrative for Bloom Energy in 2025-2026. The company has repositioned itself as a primary power solution for AI data centers facing acute grid constraints:

- **The Problem:** AI training clusters require 50-200+ MW of continuous, reliable power. Grid interconnection timelines have stretched to 3-7 years in many regions. Power availability has become the #1 bottleneck for AI infrastructure buildout.
- **Bloom's Solution:** On-site, behind-the-meter power generation that can be deployed in 55-90 days, bypassing grid queues entirely.
- **800V DC Solution [2026-02-12 Update]:** Bloom has introduced an 800-volt DC power architecture as its new product standard. All new server shipments are 800V DC ready (with removable adapter for existing AC environments), connecting fuel cells directly to AI server racks for higher efficiency, reduced waste heat, and smaller footprint. This positions Bloom ahead of the expected industry shift to 800V DC in data centers.
- **Key Partnerships:**
  - **Brookfield Asset Management:** $5B partnership to deploy fuel cells at AI data centers globally (encompassing 140 data center and industrial real estate projects [2026-02-13])
  - **AEP:** $2.65B agreement for 1 GW of SOFCs with 20-year off-take
  - **Oracle:** Powering OCI data centers with rapid deployment
  - **CoreWeave:** On-site power for high-performance AI compute
  - **Equinix:** Expanded relationship exceeding 100 MW

## 2. Industry & Competitive Position

### Total Addressable Market (TAM)

The global fuel cell market was ~$9B in 2024, projected to reach $34B by 2033 (CAGR ~15%). However, Bloom's real TAM is better framed through the data center power lens:

- Global data center power demand is expected to double from ~50 GW (2024) to 100+ GW by 2028
- On-site/distributed generation is capturing an increasing share as grid constraints worsen
- Bloom's addressable data center power market alone could be $50B+ over the next decade

### Market Share & Competitive Position

Bloom holds a **44% global market share** in stationary fuel cells â€” a dominant position. The top 4 players (Bloom, Doosan-HyAxiom, FuelCell Energy, Panasonic) control ~85% of installed capacity.

### Competitive Landscape

| **Battery Storage** | Lithium-ion | Declining costs | Duration limited (4-8 hrs), not baseload |

### Moat Assessment: ðŸ  Moderate

- **Technology moat:** Proprietary SOFC technology with 20+ years of R&D. Difficult to replicate quickly.
- **Switching costs:** Once installed, customers are locked into 10-20 year service contracts.
- **Scale advantage:** Largest SOFC manufacturer globally; manufacturing learning curve benefits.
- **Weaknesses:** Natural gas dependency creates commodity exposure; technology could be disrupted by cheaper alternatives (SMRs, advanced batteries).

4. **Natural gas dependency is a double-edged sword.** Bloom's fuel cells run primarily on natural gas. Rising gas prices directly impact customer economics. Environmental concerns about methane emissions could also create regulatory headwinds, undermining the "clean energy" narrative.

5. **Manufacturing scaling is unproven at this pace.** Doubling capacity from 1 GW to 2 GW in one year is ambitious. Supply chain constraints, quality control issues, or construction delays could cause Bloom to miss delivery commitments.

6. **Competition is intensifying.** Small modular reactors (SMRs), advanced battery storage, and even traditional gas turbines are all targeting the same data center power market. If grid interconnection timelines improve or alternative technologies mature faster, Bloom's value proposition weakens.

## 7. Key Uncertainties

### What We Don't Know

1. **AI CapEx durability:** Will hyperscalers continue spending at current rates through 2027-2028? A slowdown would directly impact Bloom's order pipeline. We'll have better visibility after Q2-Q3 2026 earnings from major cloud providers.

2. **Manufacturing execution:** Can Bloom actually double capacity to 2 GW on schedule? No public company has scaled SOFC manufacturing at this pace. First real test comes in H2 2026 delivery volumes.

3. **Service cost trajectory:** As the installed base grows, stack replacement costs (every 5-7 years) could become a significant drag. The $14B service backlog is both an asset and a liability â€” if service margins compress, it's a problem.

4. **Hydrogen timeline:** The electrolyzer business is a potential game-changer but remains pre-revenue at scale. The Jeju Island demo is a proof of concept, not a commercial validation.

5. **Regulatory environment:** IRA tax credits (45X manufacturing credit) are a meaningful tailwind. Any policy reversal could impact economics.

### Thesis-Breaking Events

- **Bull case invalid if:** AI CapEx cycle peaks in 2026, grid interconnection timelines shorten dramatically, or a competing technology (SMRs, batteries) achieves cost parity with faster deployment.
- **Bear case invalid if:** Bloom consistently beats revenue/margin guidance, backlog continues growing at 100%+ rates, and the company demonstrates manufacturing scalability without quality issues.
